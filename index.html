🧠 Erik Erikson: Psychosocial Development

🚶‍♀️

Big Idea

People develop across the life course by facing social and emotional challenges. Each stage involves a key task, such as developing trust, identity, intimacy, or purpose.

🪞

Key Question

How does family support or interfere with a person’s development of identity, confidence, intimacy, and meaning?

Visual Shortcut: Erikson is a staircase. Each step represents a developmental challenge shaped by relationships and social expectations.
Click each stage card to flip it and see the family connection.
Family Example: A teenager negotiating identity may rely on family support, peer groups, school culture, and media messages to understand who they are becoming.
Family Book Prompt: Which stage of life has been most important in shaping your family roles, relationships, or expectations?

🌍 Urie Bronfenbrenner: Ecological Systems Theory

🎯

Big Idea

Human development is shaped by nested systems: family, school, peers, workplaces, community, culture, laws, and historical time.

🧅

Patterns

Families develop repeated patterns: who comforts, who avoids conflict, who makes decisions, and who carries stress.

⚙️

Roles

Family members may take on roles such as caretaker, peacekeeper, rule follower, rebel, or emotional support person.

🌊

Ripple Effect

A change in one person’s health, work, behavior, or mood can ripple through the whole family.

Family Example: If one parent loses a job, responsibilities, emotions, routines, and conflict patterns may shift for everyone.
Family Book Prompt: What roles or patterns have repeated across your family system?

🕰️ Family Development Theory

Family development theory focuses on how families move through stages and transitions over time. Families change as people enter, leave, grow older, marry, divorce, parent, retire, or take on caregiving roles.

👶

Beginning

Couple formation, early marriage, cohabitation, new parenting, and establishing roles.

🏠

Middle Years

Raising children, work-family balance, school involvement, caregiving, and household routines.

🌅

Later Life

Launching children, aging, grandparenting, retirement, illness, grief, and legacy.

🌿 Ecological Theory

Ecological theory examines how families interact with their environments. Family life is shaped by neighborhoods, schools, work, healthcare, transportation, safety, and access to resources.

🏘️

Place Matters

Where a family lives can affect education, safety, support networks, and opportunity.

🧰

Resources Matter

Income, childcare, healthcare, time, transportation, and social support shape family stress and stability.

🌦️

Stressors Matter

Environmental pressure can come from poverty, discrimination, unsafe housing, disasters, or unstable employment.

Family Book Prompt: What environments helped or challenged your family?

🌺 Feminist Theory

Feminist theory examines how gender, power, patriarchy, unpaid labor, caregiving, and social inequality shape family experiences.

🧹

Unpaid Labor

Cooking, cleaning, planning, childcare, and emotional support are essential but often undervalued.

⚖️

Power

Family decisions may reflect unequal access to income, authority, time, safety, or respect.

🌈

Intersectionality

Gender interacts with race, class, sexuality, age, disability, and culture.

Family Example: A mother may work full time and still be expected to manage most household labor and emotional caregiving.
Family Book Prompt: How were caregiving, chores, or decision-making divided in your family?

🌸 Feminist Theory

Big Idea: Feminist theory examines how gender, power, opportunity, and inequality shape family life and human development.

🏠 Family Example

Feminist theorists might ask why women often perform more household labor even when both partners work outside the home. They examine how social expectations and gender norms influence family roles and opportunities.

📖 Apply to Your Family Book

Think about the division of household labor in your family. Were responsibilities divided equally? Were some chores viewed as "men's work" or "women's work"? How did those expectations influence family relationships?

🏛️ Structural Functionalism

Structural functionalism views the family as a major social institution that helps society continue and remain stable.

❤️

Support

Families provide care, belonging, emotional support, and socialization.

📚

Socialization

Families teach norms, values, language, roles, and expected behaviors.

🔗

Institutional Connection

Families connect to education, economy, religion, law, healthcare, and government.

Functionalist Question: What function does this family pattern serve for individuals, families, or society?
Family Book Prompt: How did your family provide stability, support, or socialization?

⚖️ Conflict Theory

Conflict theory focuses on power, inequality, scarce resources, and competing interests. Families are shaped by economic systems, social class, gender inequality, racism, and unequal access to opportunity.

💰

Resources

Money, housing, healthcare, time, and education are not equally available to all families.

📉

Stress

Inequality can increase stress, conflict, insecurity, and limited choices.

🧱

Barriers

Families may face barriers linked to class, gender, race, immigration status, age, or disability.

Conflict Question: Who has power, who lacks power, and how are resources distributed?
Family Book Prompt: How did inequality or access to resources shape your family’s experiences?

💬 Symbolic Interactionism

Symbolic interactionism focuses on meaning-making in everyday life. Families create meanings through communication, rituals, labels, roles, and repeated interactions.

🏷️

Labels

Words like “good parent,” “black sheep,” “provider,” or “responsible one” shape identity and behavior.

🍽️

Rituals

Meals, holidays, routines, jokes, stories, and traditions create family meaning.

🗣️

Interaction

Families define love, discipline, respect, success, and responsibility through daily interaction.

Interactionist Question: What does this behavior mean to the people involved?
Family Book Prompt: What words, rituals, or stories shaped your family identity?

🤝 Social Exchange Theory

Social exchange theory examines how people make relationship decisions by considering rewards, costs, expectations, fairness, and alternatives.

🎁

Rewards

Love, support, companionship, security, approval, shared goals, and emotional connection.

🧾

Costs

Time, conflict, stress, sacrifice, unequal labor, loss of freedom, or emotional strain.

⚖️

Fairness

People often evaluate whether a relationship feels balanced, reciprocal, and worth maintaining.

🧩 Applying Theories to the Family Book Project

For stronger sociology writing, do not just name a theory. Use the theory to explain something.

Instead of...Try...
“This is conflict theory.”“Conflict theory helps explain how unequal access to money and power increased stress in this family.”
“My family has roles.”“Family systems theory helps explain how each person’s role affected the entire family system.”
“My parents taught me values.”“Symbolic interactionism helps explain how daily conversations and family rituals created shared meanings about responsibility.”
Quick Writing Formula

Concept/theory + definition + family example + explanation. Example: “Family systems theory views the family as an interconnected system. In my family, one person’s stress affected everyone’s routines, emotions, and responsibilities.”

Quick Self-Check
  • Did I define the theory?
  • Did I apply it to a real or fictional family example?
  • Did I explain why the theory helps us understand the example?
  • Did I cite the textbook when using course concepts?

🌳 Final Thought

Family theories help us see that personal experiences are connected to larger patterns: development, systems, culture, institutions, inequality, interaction, and social change.
